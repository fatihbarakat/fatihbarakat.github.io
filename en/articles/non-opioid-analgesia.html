Non-Opioid Analgesia Outperforms Opioids After Wisdom-Tooth Removal

July 22, 2026

A large randomized clinical trial published in 2025 found that a combination of ibuprofen and acetaminophen provided better early pain relief and greater patient satisfaction than hydrocodone with acetaminophen after impacted mandibular third-molar extraction. This finding challenges the routine use of opioids after wisdom-tooth surgery and supports non-opioid analgesics as first-line treatment.

A clinically important comparison

Surgical removal of impacted mandibular third molars commonly produces acute inflammatory pain. Opioid-containing medications have traditionally been prescribed after this procedure, despite concerns about adverse effects, dependence, diversion, and the availability of effective non-opioid alternatives [1, 2].

The Opioid Analgesic Reduction Study, or OARS, directly compared these treatment strategies in a multisite, double-blind randomized clinical trial involving 1,815 adults undergoing impacted mandibular third-molar extraction [3]. Participants received either 400 mg of ibuprofen combined with 500 mg of acetaminophen or 5 mg of hydrocodone combined with 300 mg of acetaminophen. Medication was taken every four to six hours as needed.

Better pain control without an opioid

Patients receiving ibuprofen and acetaminophen reported significantly less pain during the first postoperative day and night. The mean difference on a 0–10 numerical pain scale was -0.70 points. The non-opioid group also experienced less pain during the second postoperative day and night [3].

Across the entire postoperative period, hydrocodone was not superior at any measured time. Patient satisfaction was also higher in the non-opioid group: 85.3% of participants reported being satisfied or extremely satisfied, compared with 78.9% in the opioid group [3].

Why the combination works

Postoperative dental pain is largely driven by tissue injury and inflammation. Ibuprofen inhibits cyclooxygenase enzymes and reduces the production of inflammatory prostaglandins. Acetaminophen acts through partly distinct central analgesic mechanisms. Combining the two drugs therefore targets pain through complementary pathways without introducing an opioid [1].

Earlier systematic evidence had already suggested that ibuprofen combined with acetaminophen was among the most effective pharmacological options following surgical dental extraction [2]. The OARS trial strengthens this conclusion by providing a large, direct comparison with a commonly prescribed opioid regimen.

Clinical implications

The findings indicate that routine opioid prescribing after wisdom-tooth surgery is generally unnecessary. For medically suitable patients, an NSAID such as ibuprofen, alone or combined with acetaminophen, should normally be considered before an opioid. This conclusion is consistent with current evidence-based guidelines for managing acute dental pain [4].

Nevertheless, the principal conclusion is clear: after impacted wisdom-tooth removal, effective pain management does not necessarily require a stronger or more addictive medication. In a large randomized trial, the non-opioid combination provided better early pain control and greater patient satisfaction.
