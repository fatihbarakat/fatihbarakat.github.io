Why a Numb Lip Can Still Mean a Painful Tooth: The Science of Failed Dental Anaesthesia

June 2, 2026 4 min read

The injection is given. The lip feels numb, the cheek feels heavy, and the tongue may feel strange. Then the dentist touches the tooth, and pain is still there. This experience can feel confusing, but it is a recognised problem in dentistry. A numb lip does not always mean a numb tooth.

The problem is especially common in a tooth with symptomatic irreversible pulpitis, often called a "hot tooth." In this condition, the pulp inside the tooth is inflamed, painful, and biologically sensitised. The British Dental Journal review by Virdee, Seymour, and Bhakta describes the acutely inflamed mandibular molar as one of the most difficult anaesthetic challenges in dentistry, with conventional techniques reported to succeed only 20–50% of the time [1]. Other reviews similarly describe the difficulty of achieving profound anaesthesia in irreversible pulpitis [2], [3].

The first key idea is that soft-tissue anaesthesia and pulpal anaesthesia are not the same. Lip numbness mainly tells us that soft tissues supplied by the nerve are affected. It does not prove that the tooth pulp is fully anaesthetised. Virdee and colleagues note that in one clinical trial all participants had profound lip numbness after an inferior alveolar nerve block, yet only about half of healthy molars achieved pulpal anaesthesia; in acutely inflamed mandibular molars, this fell to about 20% [1].

The second key idea is inflammation. Local anaesthetics are weak bases and must cross the nerve membrane before blocking sodium channels from inside the nerve cell. Inflamed tissues can alter the chemical environment around nerves, and inflammatory acidosis has long been discussed as one reason local anaesthetic action becomes less predictable [4]. However, this explanation is not complete, especially for inferior alveolar nerve blocks, where the anaesthetic is deposited away from the inflamed pulp [1].

The third key idea is nerve sensitisation. In an inflamed pulp, pain fibres are not behaving normally. Inflammatory mediators can lower the activation threshold of nociceptors, increase sodium-channel activity, and make pain signals easier to trigger. The BDJ review argues that altered nociceptor physiology is one of the strongest explanations for anaesthetic failure in the hot tooth [1]. This means the tooth may continue to send pain signals even when some anaesthetic effect is present.

There are also anatomical and technical factors. The inferior alveolar nerve block is a demanding injection, and mandibular molars are harder to anaesthetise than many upper teeth because the mandible has dense cortical bone. Accessory innervation, variable nerve anatomy, and incomplete spread of anaesthetic can all contribute. Still, the evidence reviewed by Virdee and colleagues suggests that failed anaesthesia in the hot tooth cannot be explained only by poor injection technique [1].

When the standard block does not work, dentists have other options. Repeating the same injection may not be enough. Supplemental techniques include buccal infiltration, intraosseous injection, intraligamentary injection, and intrapulpal anaesthesia when appropriate. Kanaa and colleagues compared different supplemental techniques after failed inferior alveolar nerve block in mandibular teeth with irreversible pulpitis and found that articaine buccal infiltration and intraosseous injection were more effective than simply repeating the block in that setting [5]. A systematic review also found that articaine may offer an advantage over lidocaine as a supplemental infiltration after mandibular block anaesthesia [6].

There is one more practical distinction: pain is not the same as pressure. Local anaesthesia is mainly intended to block pain. During dental treatment, pressure, vibration, pushing, or movement may still be felt. Sharp, electric, burning, or clearly painful sensation is different and should be reported immediately.

The useful lesson is simple: failed dental anaesthesia does not necessarily mean the dentist made a mistake, and it does not mean the patient is imagining the pain. In many cases, the biology of the inflamed tooth is the real problem. The hot tooth is chemically irritated, neurologically sensitised, and sometimes anatomically difficult to numb.

The home takeaway is that patients should describe what they feel precisely. "Pressure" and "sharp pain" are different clinical messages. If sharp pain remains, treatment should not simply continue as though nothing is happening. The dentist can wait longer, test pulpal anaesthesia more carefully, or use supplemental anaesthetic techniques. The interesting point is that the mouth can feel numb while the tooth is still awake, because soft tissues and inflamed pulp do not always become anaesthetised at the same depth, speed, or reliability.
