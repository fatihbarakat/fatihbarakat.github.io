Bleeding Gums, Bone Signals, and Saliva: What Wnt5a and LRP5 Reveal About Periodontitis

July 2, 2026 4 min read

A gum that bleeds during brushing may look like a small problem, but it can be the visible surface of a deeper inflammatory process. Periodontitis is not only a matter of plaque on teeth; it is a disease in which bacterial biofilm, immune response, soft-tissue damage, and bone loss meet in the same biological space. In its advanced forms, especially Stage III periodontitis, the supporting structures of the teeth are already seriously affected [1].

This is why saliva has become an attractive diagnostic fluid. It is easy to collect, painless, and close to the site of disease. Instead of seeing saliva only as a fluid in the mouth, researchers increasingly view it as a biochemical snapshot of inflammation, tissue breakdown, and bone remodeling [2], [3].

The study Expression of Wnt5a and WNT Coreceptor (LRP5) Levels in Stage III Periodontitis Patients investigated two molecules connected to these processes: Wnt5a and LRP5 [4]. Wnt5a belongs to the Wnt signaling family and has been associated with inflammatory activity in periodontal tissues. LRP5 is a Wnt co-receptor with an important connection to bone biology. Together, they are interesting because periodontitis is both an inflammatory disease and a bone-destructive disease.

Earlier work supports this direction. Nanbara and colleagues showed that Wnt5a expression can be increased in chronic periodontitis tissues and can be stimulated by periodontopathic bacteria such as Porphyromonas gingivalis [5]. More recent work by Güney and colleagues found altered Wnt signaling in Stage III periodontitis tissues, including increased Wnt3a, Wnt5a, and Wnt10b expression compared with controls [6]. Chatzopoulos and colleagues also reported that WNT-5a levels in gingival crevicular fluid vary with periodontal inflammatory and osteoclastogenic activity [7].

The Wnt5a/LRP5 study asked whether these molecules could be detected differently in people with Stage III periodontitis compared with periodontally healthy controls. The reported finding was that salivary Wnt5a and LRP5 levels were higher in the periodontitis group [4]. This does not prove that these molecules cause periodontitis. Rather, it suggests that they may be part of the molecular environment that accompanies severe periodontal inflammation and bone loss.

The most interesting point is not only the molecules themselves, but where they were measured. If saliva can reflect inflammatory and bone-related signals, then future periodontal diagnosis may become less dependent on damage that is already visible. Clinical probing and radiographs will remain essential, but biomarkers may one day help identify active disease, monitor treatment response, or distinguish stable tissue from tissue that is still biologically inflamed.

For now, the message is simpler. Bleeding gums should not be treated as normal. Interdental cleaning matters because toothbrushes do not fully clean the spaces between teeth, where biofilm can remain. A large population-based study found that the association between interdental cleaning and lower periodontitis risk was clearer among adults with at least 20 remaining teeth [8].

The interesting lesson from Wnt5a and LRP5 research is that periodontitis is not just a surface problem. It leaves molecular traces. Saliva may carry clues about inflammation, tissue remodeling, and bone loss before these changes become obvious in daily life. The home takeaway is direct: bleeding, swelling, recession, loose teeth, and persistent bad breath deserve attention; daily interdental cleaning and regular periodontal checkups remain the most useful tools available today.
